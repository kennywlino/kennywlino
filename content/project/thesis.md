### **Abstract**

While learners may be taught vocabulary, grammar, socio-cultural knowledge and other higher-pillared aspects of language due to the emphasis in foreign language (FL) education on language function over language form, pronunciation tends to take the backseat for a numerous amount of reasons, including time and a lack of resources. Perhaps even more neglected within pronunciation are the prosodic features of language (i.e. stress, rhythm and intonation), even though they serve to help organize our utterances and implicitly communicate information about ourselves (Lengeris, 2012). Pronunciation has also been said to play a role in a speaker’s intelligibility, comprehensibility and fluency (Munro and Derwing, 1995). Despite the evidence that points to the need to integrate pronunciation instruction, learners are still not given the attention or tools needed to improve their pronunciation. Although the communicative approach to language teaching has led to greater success and acquisition of language overall, the lack of pronunciation instruction in this approach is perhaps somewhat ironic considering the role of pronunciation in communication. In this thesis, I propose a tool to help learners autonomously learn better prosody by providing good exemplars and appropriate feedback mechanisms to reinforce their learning.
